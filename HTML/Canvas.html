Explanation

You can see that we made something weird with the context and the drawing of planets and satellites. We don't place them like they position themselves around the sun. We place them on an axis, then we move the context in a way that the upper left corner of the context in which we drew our objects is in the center of the visible frame. We do this because the angle around which the rotation will occur is the upper left corner of the context.

The planet and its satellite are not placed in relation to their final position or to the sun's position.

The variable "i" is a variable which will decrement each turn, it's with this variable that we are going to modify the rotation of the context.

The animate() function :

  • Framework clearing ;
  • Black background setup ;
  • Drawing of the sun and the planet .

As you can see, we multiply the planets's speed by the variable "i", this allows to have a value for the angle.

To draw the planet, we work in a different context from that in whichwe draw the sun but we restore it when we have finished drawing the planet.

Drawing of the second planet and its satellite. Decrementation of "i" & end of the animate() function : we use a save(), while we have not restored the base context. So it's possible to use many saves and many restore. It's a system is said "by stacking". It means that when we use restore(), it is in relation to the last context that was saved.


Advanced simulation


Here you can see a more advanced simulation of the solar system, and the Mercury's rather eccentric orbit is drawn ( eccentricity : 0.205630 ). Its orbital eccentricity is the largest of all known planets in the Solar System


The planets are, in order from nearest to farthest of the sun : Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

Pluto is not drawn as it is not considered a planet anymore, but a Dwarf planet.
